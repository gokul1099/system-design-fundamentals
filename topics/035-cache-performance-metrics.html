Cache Performance Metrics

⏱ 4 min read 🟡 Intermediate Caching Metrics Performance

Core Concept

When implementing caching, it’s important to measure the performance of the cache to ensure that it is effective in reducing latency and improving system performance. Here are some of the most common cache performance metrics:

Hit rate

The hit rate is the percentage of requests that are served by the cache without accessing the original source. A high hit rate indicates that the cache is effective in reducing the number of requests to the original source, while a low hit rate indicates that the cache may not be providing significant performance benefits.

Miss rate

The miss rate is the percentage of requests that are not served by the cache and need to be fetched from the original source. A high miss rate indicates that the cache may not be caching the right data or that the cache size may not be large enough to store all frequently accessed data.

Cache size

The cache size is the amount of memory or storage allocated for the cache. The cache size can impact the hit rate and miss rate of the cache. A larger cache size can result in a higher hit rate, but it may also increase the cost and complexity of the caching solution.

Cache latency

The cache latency is the time it takes to access data from the cache. A lower cache latency indicates that the cache is faster and more effective in reducing latency and improving system performance. The cache latency can be impacted by the caching technology used, the cache size, and the cache replacement and invalidation policies.

Beginner Analogy

💡
Cache performance metrics are like the stats a store manager tracks for their stockroom. Cache hit ratio is how often a customer finds what they want on the shelf versus having to wait for a warehouse order. Latency is how long that shelf lookup takes. Eviction rate tells you how often items are being removed to make room for new ones. Together these metrics tell you whether your stockroom strategy is actually working.

Real-World Examples

🎬 Netflix
Netflix monitors cache hit ratio per content type — popular current season episodes should achieve 95%+ hit rates on CDN edges, while long-tail catalog items may hit only 40-50%. When hit ratios drop unexpectedly, it signals either a cache misconfiguration or a traffic pattern shift. SREs use these metrics to tune TTLs and cache capacity.
☁️ Cloudflare
Cloudflare publishes aggregate cache hit ratios as part of their performance dashboards for customers. A customer with a low hit ratio is coached to add cache-control headers, increase TTLs, or restructure URLs to improve cacheability. Hit ratio improvements directly reduce origin load and improve response times.

Interview Tips

  • 💡 Lead with hit ratio as the primary health metric — a high hit ratio means the cache is effectively serving traffic and reducing backend load.
  • 💡 Explain that a hit ratio below 70-80% often signals that the cache is too small, TTLs are too short, or the data being cached is not frequently accessed.
  • 💡 Discuss eviction rate: high eviction rates suggest the cache is at capacity and items are being removed before they can be reused — a signal to increase cache size.
  • 💡 Mention that both hit ratio and latency should be tracked per cache segment (hot vs. warm data) rather than as a single average, since averages can hide problems in specific data categories.

Key Takeaways

✅ Cache hit ratio is the most important metric: it measures what percentage of requests are served from cache.
✅ A high eviction rate signals that the cache is undersized for the working set of data.
✅ Cache latency should be consistently low (1-5ms for in-memory caches); spikes indicate contention or memory pressure.
✅ Memory utilization should be maintained below 80% to prevent thrashing and allow for traffic spikes.
✅ Monitoring cache metrics per data type or endpoint reveals which content is well-cached and which needs tuning.
