Introduction to System Design

⏱ 4 min read 🟢 Beginner Overview Fundamentals

Core Concept

To excel in system design, one of the most crucial aspects is to develop a deep understanding of fundamental system design concepts such as Load Balancing, Caching, Partitioning, Replication, Databases, and Proxies.

This course will go through key concepts that can make a significant difference in your ability to tackle system design problems. These concepts range from understanding the intricacies of API Gateway and mastering Load Balancing techniques to grasping the importance of CDNs and appreciating the role of Caching in modern distributed systems. By the end of this course, you’ll have a comprehensive understanding of these essential ideas and the confidence to apply them in your next interview.

When discussing system design interviews, it's important to note that they are unstructured by nature. During the interview, it can be challenging to maintain focus and ensure all crucial elements of the design are addressed. This course should guide you in answering any system design interview question by making sure that you have not missed discussing any critical aspect.

Let's get started.

Beginner Analogy

💡
Think of system design like architecting a city. You need roads (networks), buildings (servers), water systems (databases), and emergency services (fault tolerance) all working together. Just as a city planner must anticipate growth and failure, a system designer must plan for scale, reliability, and change from day one.

Real-World Examples

🎬 Netflix
Netflix designed its system to stream video to over 200 million subscribers across 190 countries simultaneously. Their architecture uses microservices, CDNs, and chaos engineering to ensure movies keep playing even when servers fail.
📦 Amazon
Amazon's e-commerce platform processes millions of orders per day, especially during peak events like Prime Day. Their system design separates concerns into hundreds of independent services, each ownable by a small team.
🔍 Google
Google Search must return results in under 200ms while querying petabytes of indexed web data. This requires a carefully designed distributed system with caching layers, parallel query execution, and globally distributed data centers.

Interview Tips

  • 💡 Always clarify requirements before drawing any diagrams — ask about expected users, read/write ratio, and consistency needs to avoid designing the wrong system.
  • 💡 Use the REQS framework: Requirements, Estimates, entities (data model), Query design, and Scale to structure your answer logically.
  • 💡 State your assumptions out loud so the interviewer can correct you early, saving time and showing collaborative thinking.
  • 💡 Start with a simple design that works, then iteratively add complexity to handle scale, fault tolerance, and edge cases.

Key Takeaways

✅ System design is the process of defining architecture, components, and data flow to satisfy functional and non-functional requirements.
✅ Non-functional requirements (NFRs) like latency, availability, and throughput often drive the hardest architectural decisions.
✅ There is no perfect system design — every choice is a trade-off between competing constraints such as consistency vs availability.
✅ Understanding bottlenecks and failure modes is as important as designing the happy path.
✅ Iterative refinement, starting simple and adding complexity only when justified, produces more maintainable systems.
